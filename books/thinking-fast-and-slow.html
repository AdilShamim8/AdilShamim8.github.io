Psychology & Decision Making

Thinking, Fast and Slow

by Daniel Kahneman — Read in 2026

Year Read: 2026
Must Read

Learning Summary

What this book is really about

Nobel Prize winner Daniel Kahneman presents decades of research into how we think. His central framework: we have two cognitive systems. System 1 is fast, intuitive, emotional, and automatic. System 2 is slow, deliberate, logical, and effortful. Most of our thinking is System 1 on autopilot — and this leads to predictable, systematic errors called cognitive biases.

For an ML engineer, this book is doubly relevant. It explains the psychology of human decision-making (useful when building AI products) and it exposes the biases that can infect ML model evaluation — anchoring, availability heuristic, overconfidence — things I had to consciously guard against in my own Kaggle experiments.

"Nothing in life is as important as you think it is, while you are thinking about it."

Key Lessons

5 ideas that changed how I evaluate decisions

1
System 1 is in charge far more than you think
We believe we're making conscious, rational decisions. We're usually not. System 1 makes a snap judgment and System 2 rationalizes it after the fact. Recognizing when you're using System 1 vs System 2 is the first step to better decisions.
2
Cognitive biases are not flaws — they're features gone wrong
Anchoring, availability heuristic, loss aversion — these exist because they were once useful. Understanding the mechanism of each bias is more useful than knowing they exist, because you can then design checklists and processes to compensate for them.
3
We are loss-averse, not just risk-averse
Losses feel about twice as painful as equivalent gains feel good. This has huge implications for product design (don't frame things as losses), investment decisions, and why people hold losing stocks too long. I now frame ML model improvements in gain terms, not risk terms, when presenting results.
4
Overconfidence is the most dangerous bias for experts
Experts are often overconfident in their predictions, especially in complex domains. Kahneman's research shows that algorithms consistently outperform human expert predictions in many domains. This reinforced my belief in measuring everything and trusting data over intuition.
5
The experiencing self vs the remembering self
We don't remember experiences accurately — we remember the peak and the end. This explains why a great conference with one bad session feels like a "bad conference." For product design: the end of the user journey matters disproportionately for satisfaction and retention.

My Honest Opinion

Should you read it?

Adil's Verdict
Must Read

Thinking, Fast and Slow is dense and long (~500 pages), but the core ideas are profound enough to justify the investment. The research is rigorous, the examples are memorable, and Kahneman writes with unusual honesty about the limits of his own work.

It's not a quick read and parts of the middle drag. I'd recommend reading the first three parts, skimming the later chapters, and coming back to the sections most relevant to your work.

Particularly valuable for ML engineers — understanding bias in human judgment makes you better at building AI systems that augment human decision-making without inheriting human blind spots.

Read this if you are…

Working on AI/ML products where you need to understand human decision-making
Interested in why smart people make systematic, predictable errors
A data scientist — the chapters on statistical thinking are exceptional
Looking for a short, actionable read — this is a long academic book in accessible form
