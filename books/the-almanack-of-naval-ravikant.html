Philosophy & Wealth

The Almanack of Naval Ravikant

by Eric Jorgenson — Read in 2024

Year Read: 2024
Read Time: ~5 hours
My Rating: 9.5 / 10
Must Read

Learning Summary

What this book is really about

This is not a typical business book. It's a curated collection of Naval Ravikant's tweets, podcasts, and essays — organized by Eric Jorgenson into a coherent life philosophy. Naval is an Indian-American entrepreneur and investor (co-founder of AngelList) who has spent decades thinking rigorously about wealth, happiness, and how to live a meaningful life.

The book is split into two parts: Wealth and Happiness. The wealth section is not about making money for its own sake — it's about achieving financial independence so that you can spend your time on things you actually care about.

What makes Naval's thinking exceptional is his ability to compress complex ideas into short, precise principles. This book is a masterclass in first-principles thinking applied to life.

"Seek wealth, not money or status. Wealth is having assets that earn while you sleep. Money is how we transfer time and wealth. Status is your place in the social hierarchy."

Key Lessons

5 ideas I carry everywhere

1
Leverage is everything in the modern world
Naval identifies three types of leverage: labor (people working for you), capital (money), and products (code & media that scale with zero marginal cost). Code and content are the new leverage — they work while you sleep. This is why I invest in building open-source ML tools and writing publicly.
2
Specific knowledge cannot be taught
Specific knowledge is found at the intersection of what you're genuinely curious about, what you're good at, and what the world values. It can't be learned from a textbook — it's found by obsessively pursuing your own curiosity. For me, that's Bengali speech AI and low-resource NLP.
3
Play long-term games with long-term people
Compound interest works in relationships, knowledge, and trust — not just finance. Every interaction with a collaborator or employer is either building or eroding trust. People who understand compounding build reputations that do work on their behalf.
4
Learn to sell AND build — then you're unstoppable
Most engineers can build but not sell. The rare combination of both — making something AND communicating its value — is historically the most powerful position. Engineering is my building. Writing and open source is my selling.
5
Happiness is a skill, not a destination
Naval argues that happiness is not something you achieve — it's something you practice. He defines it as the absence of desire, not the fulfilment of desire. This has genuinely changed how I measure personal success.

My Honest Opinion

Should you read it?

Adil's Verdict
Must Read

This book reframed how I think about my entire career. I stopped optimizing for salary and started optimizing for leverage, learning, and long-term compounding. It's why I build open-source tools, write publicly, and chase problems that genuinely excite me.

The writing is incredibly dense — each sentence punches above its weight. You can read the whole book in a weekend, but you'll still be finding new implications six months later.

If you only read one non-technical book this year, make it this one. It's free online, it's short, and it will change how you think about wealth, time, and happiness.

Read this if you are…

An engineer who wants to understand how to build financial freedom, not just a job
Someone who feels stuck in the rat race and wants a different mental model
A builder who's struggling to articulate why they work on what they work on
Someone looking for step-by-step investment tactics — this is philosophy, not a how-to guide
Someone wanting a continuous narrative — it's fragmented by design, which some readers find jarring
