History & Humanity

Sapiens

by Yuval Noah Harari — Read in 2025

Year Read: 2025
My Rating: 9.5 / 10
Must Read

Learning Summary

What this book is really about

Sapiens is a sweeping history of humankind from the Stone Age to the present. Harari's central question: how did Homo sapiens — a relatively weak animal — come to dominate every ecosystem on earth?

The answer, Harari argues, is our unique ability to believe in shared fictions — money, nations, religions, companies — that allow millions of strangers to cooperate at scale. No other animal can do this.

As an ML engineer building AI systems, the final chapters on the upcoming cognitive revolution — where AI and biotech may replace or enhance Homo sapiens — felt less like speculation and more like a checklist of things I'm already working on.

"History is something that very few people have been doing while everyone else was ploughing fields and carrying water."

Key Lessons

4 ideas that expanded my thinking

1
Shared fictions are humanity's superpower
Money, nations, religions, and companies are all shared fictions — they don't exist in nature, but they allow millions of strangers to cooperate. Understanding this changes how you see every institution around you, including the tech companies we build and the AI systems we deploy.
2
The Cognitive Revolution unlocked everything
Around 70,000 years ago, Sapiens developed the ability to think in abstractions. This let us form large social groups and outcompete every other species. Language and storytelling are the original disruptive technologies.
3
Progress always has victims
The Agricultural Revolution, often celebrated as progress, actually made early farmers work harder and eat worse than their hunter-gatherer ancestors. Every technological revolution creates winners and losers. As engineers building AI, this is a sobering reminder to think about who bears the cost.
4
We are living through the most transformative period in history
Harari argues we are on the cusp of replacing Homo sapiens through biotech and AI. Working in ML gives these final chapters a different weight — it's not abstract future speculation, it's my daily work.

My Honest Opinion

Should you read it?

Adil's Verdict
Must Read

Sapiens is the best single-volume overview of human history I've read. Harari writes with remarkable clarity and provokes genuine discomfort — the mark of an important book.

Its greatest strength is zoom level: it moves from individual genetics to civilisational patterns effortlessly, connecting biology, economics, and anthropology into one coherent narrative.

Essential reading for anyone in technology — understanding where humanity came from is essential context for building where it's going.

Read this if you are…

Anyone curious about why the world is the way it is — this is the best starting point
AI/ML engineers thinking about the long-term implications of what they build
History purists — Harari simplifies and some academics dispute his interpretations
Looking for actionable takeaways — this is big-picture understanding, not a how-to guide
